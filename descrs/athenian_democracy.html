The advent of democracy sparked social change that transformed Athens into the preeminent city-state in Greece during the fifth-century. Much of the cultural, political, and economic legacy of classical Greece originated here such as the columned architectural style of the Parthenon, economic specialization and living above subsistance, legendary battles with Persia and Sparta, and the philosophy of Socrates and Plato. At its height, Athens was able to impose quasi-imperial rule over hundreds of Greek settlements on the Aegean coast. The city's population grew to around 300,000 while the population of the Greek world as a whole grew to nearly 10 million, comparable to modern levels.

Athens took its first steps toward democracy a century earlier with the Solonian Reforms, but an era of tyranny followed. Then in 508 BCE, the citizen body successfully resisted the installation of Spartan puppet rulers in favor of populist aristocrat Cleisthenes. Obliged to make good on his political promises, Cleisthenes continued the Solonian efforts and established the first democratic government in Greece. Decisions were made and implemented by a Council of 500 annually-appointed citizens from ten geographical regions (tribes) of Attica. Councilers were drawn from the new class of citizens which included every 18+ male resident of Attica.

Democracy was a big part of what made fifth-century Athens a period of flourishing. Once the government was wrested out of elite hands, the relationship between the state and the people became mutually beneficial. The state funded public architecture and cultural events. The construction of city walls and a navy (the largest in Greece) provided protect against invasion and facilitated trade across the Aegean by eliminating piracy. When a massive Persian invasion threatened the Greek mainland in 481 BCE, the enormous effort was taken to evacuate the citizen population of the city to safety in the Peloponnese. In return, the citizen soldiers gained a boost of motivation to defend the city since their personal well-being was tied to that of the community.

In many respects, the political histories of Rome and Athens are similar. Both began as relatively minor city-states until a political revolution enabled them to eclipse their neighbors in wealth and power and collect tribute across a wide expanse of territory. But while Athenian hegemony collapsed, Rome went on to establish a transcontinental empire. The difference might lie with each society's understanding of citizenship. Both city-states extended citizenship beyond their immediate metropolitian areas, Athens to all free men of Attica, Rome to Latins and elite Italians. However, the proliferation of citizenship never reached the numerous tributary states of the Athenian empire. The strong local identities and political affiliations of the Greek world prevented the Delian League from surpassing, in the minds of Greek subjects, a defensive military alliance. The Romans handed out citizenship much more liberally because the Roman identity was defined by militaristic traditions, which could and were adopted by others, more so than the location and ethnic makeup of Rome itself.
