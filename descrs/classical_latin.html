The spoken and written language of the Romans was Latin. Although the language evolved over the course of Roman history, the form of Latin spoken from around the first century BCE through the second century CE, dubbed "Classical Latin", has been considerd as the standard or proper form of the language, both spoken and written.

Latin is a heavily inflected language. Inflection is the use of word form, rather than word order, to convey the role of a word in a sentence. Vesigial remnants of inflection enable native English speakers to distinguish between "Him give I the book" and "He gives me the book" even though word order is fundamentally unchanged. Inflection manifests itself in two basic ways: conjugation of verbs and declension of nouns and adjectives. In modern English, conjugation only remains for simple verbs such as "be" (I am, you are, he is, etc.) and with the extra "s" for many verbs in third-person singular (I give, he gives). Declension remains for pronouns (I vs. me, he vs. him).

Verbs

The conjugation of a verb indicates the grammatical person, number, and tense, among other qualities, of the subject. Conjugations of verbs are often presented in groups of six enumerating the person and number. There are four main ways, labeled 1st through 4th, that a verb can be conjugated. Each verb is conjugated according to one of the four categories depending on the ending of its infinitive form. Nouns and Adjectives

Latin nouns and adjectives have grammatical gender, number, and case. Latin uses three genders (masculine, feminine, and neuter) and two numbers (singular and plural). The case refers to the syntactical role of a noun in a sentence. For example, a noun in the accusative case acts as direct object, and the ablative case indicates objects of prepositions (commonly from and with). Classical Latin retains five primary cases given in the table below, although vestiges of additional cases still remain. A set of suffixes, each of which signifies one of the various combinations of gender, number, and case, is called a declension. Although a single declension would suffice, Latin has five distinct declensions and various exceptions which seem to serve little purpose beyond inflating the complexity of the language.

Determining the declension and gender of a noun is not always straightforward. Nouns by themselves are generally referred to using their nominative singular form which is often enough information to determine the declension. Gender can often be learned from the declension. For example, most first and fifth declension nouns are feminine unless referencing something explicitly male, such as nauta 'sailor' and agricola 'farmer'. Most second, third, and forth declension nouns are either masculine or neuter, and the declension changes slightly depending on which it is.
