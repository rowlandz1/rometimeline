The Second Punic War is perhaps the most famous and impactful conflict in the history of the Roman Republic. The infamous Carthaginian general Hannibal came to be one of the most feared enemies for his invasion of the Italian heartland. Not since the Gallic sack two hundred years earlier had they come so close to destruction. But with unshakable, and at times downright illogical determination to continue fighting, the Romans snatched victory from the jaws of defeat. The war's conclusion left Rome the undisputed master of the Mediterranean and ushered in the rapid expansion and political evolution of the late Republican period.

The war began in Spain when Hannibal sacked the Roman-allied city of Seguntum in 219 BCE. Rome deployed the bulk of their troops to Spain and Sicily and a small defensive force to the Po Valley to deal with any opportunistic Gauls. To their surprise, the small Po Valley army would face the full force of Hannibal's invasion. War was declared in 218 BCE and Hannibal immediately set out from Saguntum.

War in Italy

Hannibal's route to Italy was fraught with danger, and his army frequently took heavy losses to maintain a rapid push through hostile territory. His passage through the Alps snow set in was especially treacherous, and it is a testament to the loyalty he commanded that his entire army didn't desert. He arrived in the Po Valley in autumn of 218 BCE with an army of about 25,000 men. The Romans were too hasty to engage Hannibal and suffered critical losses at Trebia and Lake Trasimene. Hannibal learned from these early battles that he could leverage his advantages and outsmart the Romans instead of outnumber them. The Romans, now surely panicking, appointed as dictator Quintus Fabius Maximus who decided that the best strategy was to pick his fights wisely and avoid a major defeat at all costs, which came to be known as 'Fabian tactics'.

The Romans learned the value of Fabian tactics the one major time they abandoned them. Hannibal was now running amock in Italy and Fabius's reluctance to engage was taken as a sign of cowardice. Gaius Terentius Varro was elected consul of 216 BCE and promised a more aggressive response. He raised a massive army of 80,000 men and confronted Hannibal at Cannae. Hannibal baited Varro into a direct frontal attack of his weaker infantry, and the Romans were surrounded by his Numidian cavalry. Despite the almost two-to-one troop ratio, the Romans suffered devistating losses including critical veteran leaders that nearly cost them the entire war.

The Battle of Cannae was the pinnacle of Hannibal's career. With his stunning victory, he paraded around southern Italy trying to coerce cities to defect to the Carthaginian side. The Romans, incredibly, raised entirely new legions and followed Hannibal around reasserting Roman authority and engaging in occasional indecisive skirmishes. In 207 BCE, the monotony was broken when Hasdrubal, Hannibal's brother, crossed the Alps and arrived in Italy with an army of 30,000 men intent on linking up with Hannibal. However, the Romans soon routed them in the Battle of the Metaurus. Roman control of Italy was now nearly assured, and a bitter and weary Hannibal was finally recalled to Africa in 204 BCE.

Other Fronts

Concurrent with the Italian camgaigns, significant conflict also took place in Greece, Sicily, and Spain. In Greece, Macedon under King Philip V made a treaty of mutual assistance with Hannibal and sent troops west. Rome sent envoys to the other Greek polities such as the Aetolian League to persuade them to keep Macedon occupied. The envoys were successful and Macedon was too embroiled in the First Macedonian War to be of much help to Hannibal.

In Sicily, longtime friend of Rome Hiero of Syracuse died in 215 BCE and a pro-Carthaginian faction of Syracusan leaders took control of the city. In 212 BCE, the Roman army stationed in Sicily laid siege to Syracuse which was famously defended by weapons invented by Archimedes. The defenses weren't enough though and the Romans broke through and sacked the city. Archimedes was killed in the chaos.

In Spain, Rome was able to secure the area between the Pyrenees mountains and the Ebro River preventing reinforcements from reaching Hannibal. However, the situation soon turned sour as the Roman armies suffered major defeats in 211 and 210 BCE. The Senate considered Spain to be a lost cause and focused their attention elsewhere until Scipio's victories a year later.

Scipio Africanus

The man who would turn a hopeless situation into a win was Publius Scipio. In 210 BCE, Scipio arrived in Spain with 10,000 men to try to salvage the situation there. In a bold unexected move that mirrored Hannibal's invasion of Italy, Scipio marched on New Carthage, the center of Carthaginian presence in Iberia. The city was heavily fortified and there was no way that his army would be able to maintain a long siege, but a small contingent snuck into the city through a shallow lagoon to open the gates, and the city fell. In a genius political gesture, Scipio freed the captive leaders of the local settlements prompting a wave of defection accross Iberia to the Roman side. In 206 BCE, the Carthaginians sent a large army in retaliation, but Scipio drove off his outnumbered enemy using a double flanking maneuver. The Battle of Ilipa became Scipio's version of Hannibal's victory at Cannae signaling his role as the hero of the Second Punic War.

With persuasive success under his belt in Spain, Scipio returned to Rome to propose an invasion of Africa to end the war. Though the Senate was reluctant, Scipio's charisma won out and he was granted his request. He arrived the next year and linked up with Masinissa, a Numidian prince who led a faction of cavalry disgruntled with Carthaginian rule. The Carthaginian Senate finally recalled Hannibal from Italy and ordered him to attack the Romans. The two legendary generals and their armies met at Zama. The battle that followed was an evenly-matched head-on clash, but Masinissa's cavalry and the superiority of the Roman legions carried the day. Hannibal was defeated and Carthage surrendered.

Aftermath

Scipio was granted the agnomen 'Africanus' for his decisive victory at Zama. Carthage was forced cede all of its overseas territories to Rome and comply with harsh restrictions on their military engagement. The loss of Rome's most threatening rival had vast consequences on the political evolution of the burgeoning empire. The Roman aristocracy was left with unchecked power over the Mediterranean. Scipio had such a personal gravitas that he unintentionally introduced cults of personality to Roman politics.
